Case Study: The Intersection of Climate Change and Child Marriage: Impacts on Women and Girls


Background

  • Child marriage and climate change are interconnected issues that disproportionately affect women and girls.
  • Climate change increases economic vulnerability through crop failures, water scarcity, and natural disasters, often driving families to marry off daughters early as a survival strategy.
  • Displacement and migration due to climate-related disasters heighten the risk of child marriage as families try to protect or reduce the financial burden of caring for their children.
  • This results in disrupted education, loss of autonomy, health risks due to early pregnancies, and a perpetuating cycle of poverty and inequality.
  • Addressing this requires gender-sensitive climate adaptation policies, stronger legal protections, educational support, and economic resilience programs for affected families.


Key Findings

  • Education Disruption : Climate disasters, such as floods and droughts, disrupt education by destroying schools or making it unsafe for children to travel. Families in crisis situations are more likely to withdraw girls from school, prioritizing boys’ education when resources are scarce. This limits girls' educational opportunities and increases their chances of being married early.
  • Loss of Autonomy and Health Risks : Child marriage strips girls of their autonomy, increasing their vulnerability to gender-based violence, early pregnancies, and health complications. Climate change-induced displacement or migration often results in loss of community and social support structures, which exacerbates these issues. With early pregnancies, young girls face increased health risks, including maternal mortality and complications during childbirth, which are further intensified in communities lacking healthcare due to climate disruptions.
  • Future Generational Impact : Child marriages perpetuate a cycle of poverty and limited opportunity. As the next generation of women is forced into early marriages due to climate pressures, their children also face greater health risks, lack of education, and economic deprivation. This perpetuates a cycle where each climate shock pushes more families into adopting harmful coping strategies.
